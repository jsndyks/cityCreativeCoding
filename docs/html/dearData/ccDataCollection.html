So ... how might you collect data and what we actually mean by data as you create your Dear Data inspired Dear Data 100 demos.

Basically, Data in this context is any recording made of any observation.
You should make these as easily as possible, with sufficient information to capture what has been observed. Some examples and some guidance follow.

Data Collection

What is Data?

You can collect numbers, photographs, words, anything you like really that captures useful characteristics of the phenomenon that interests you.

Just do this regularly and with consistency, although what you actually collect about the phenomena may change over time as your data collection informs your process.

Try to minimise these changes though, as you really want your data to be as consistent over time as possible, but see in one of the examples below, that we decided to collect something additional as we realised that the data that we were capturing was not sufficiently rich: were the football shirts current or vintage? After seeing a few we understood that we needed to know (or at least estimate) their age as well as the other characteristics.

Paper

Here, we are using a pen and paper to collect data - easy to carry around, quick to record.

Each row is a day, and every time I observe something that relates to the phenomenon that I am interested in, I jot it down.

This could be the colour of the traffic lights I pass: Red, Amber or Green.

It could be the emotions of the public servants I encounter (bus drivers, shop assistants, lecturers): Kind, Rude, Generous, Absent, Whatever or Angry.

But we don't collect much data during each observation, just a single code.
Can you think of additional characteristics of each observation that you might want to record to give yourself a richer data set?

There is a trade-off here that you may need to consider. If you are observing something that happens regularly you may not have the time to capture detail ... like the traffic lights example above (although you might be able to record where and when it happened, what you were traveling in and perhaps how long a Red hold lasted?). However if you have fewer observations you may wish to record more characteristics and detail to give yourselves a rich enough data set to produce interesting and rich DESCRIPTIONS that can result in meaningful DISCOVERY.

Table : Notebook

We gave you the lovely NoteBooks for a reason - to record data, sketch ideas and help you with your #CreativeCoding.

When we have more than one characteristic of an observation to record, we often use a table.

Tables can be effective for recording data if we use one row for each observation, along with columns for each characteristic of the observation.

We usually want to try to record the same characteristics (columns) each time, but sometimes during our data collection we realise that we need more information about each observation. For example, here it became apparent that the age of the shirt - the season or decade when it was worn by the team, needed to be collected as well as the date, time, football team, whether it was the home or away jersey, and the apparent gender of the wearer.

We will need to transcribe these data into a digital format at some stage, but that's not a big job and data collection is often quickest, easiest and most reliable when completed using paper and pen.

Table : Notes

This is pretty much the same thing, but digital.

Most operating systems have handy apps for quick data collection.

Notice that I am using codes here to record characteristics of each tutorial - how awake did each student seem? I try to be consistent about my judgments here, but my consistency will, to an extent depend upon how awake I am!

Spreadsheet : Excel

You should be getting the hang of this by now ... rows for observations, columns for characteristics.

Here we are collecting data about the awkward moments when lecturers say "Ummm..." in lectures. Us lecturers try not to do this but its ... Ummm .... like .... Ummm, kinda .... really hard.

Each "Ummm..." is an event that we want to capture information about, to learn more about when and how the dreaded "Ummm..." occurs.
They are particularly awful and painful if they occur right after a failed attempt at a joke by the way.

So, in this example we are collecting ...

  • codes - the initials of the lecturer, an String that uniquely and intuitively identifies them
  • dates - the day on which the "Ummm..." event occurred
  • times - the time at which the "Ummm..." was uttered
  • words - or perhaps we could call them Strings - the term, word or phrase that immediately followed the "Ummm..."
  • codes - a numeric code that tells us something subjective about how flustered and embarrassed the lecturer was when they said "Ummm..."

But of course, you decide what you record about each event, What is useful? What is interesting? What might help you with data discovery?

Photo

Your data collection might involve Photos or even Sounds - what did you see or here about a particular theme?

Processing has powerful capabilities for displaying and manipulating images, video and sound as we will discover.

Here, I have used a photo to capture some data about ... well ... the appalling garbage 'food' that my daughters managed to collect when dressed as kind of goth Olivia Rodrigo inspired witches out on the streets of our village last Halloween.

Or, the photo or audio could be your data itself ... you could record whatever is going on at 9:30 every day for a minute, or the sound in a particular place at different times of day, or the sky at 8am or the first bus that you take each morning. Be imaginative!

But also remember to be anonymous - note that I have used initials for my daughter's names above (so that nobody can identify April and November by name - D'OH!) , and for the lecturers too.
Joking aside ... Ummm ... be sure to record data in ways that do not reveal identity.
