Year 1 Personal Tutorials & #CreativeCoding

Once again we are running our #CreativeCoding activity across this term for the Year 1 CS students.
Giacomo, Golnaz and I will be leading with support in the labs from Olga, Aravin, Charlie and Sofiia.

#CreativeCoding is not a module - it is an activity, and it is not assessed - though students are encouraged to create a submission that enables them to experiment with code and data. It runs in Hijack Mode in weeks 01, 02, 07 & 08, when it uses the scheduled rooms of existing timetabled activities for lectures and practical classes (plus a couple of extra sessions too). It also aligns with and is indeed the focus for the Year 1 Personal Tutorials organised by Dha, and this document is an attempt to brief Personal Tutors on how this will work and what is required.

You will note Four Touch-Points when Personal Tutorials will connect with the #CreativeCoding activity.
After meeting tutees in induction week (week 00) there will be 2x group and 2x individual personal tutorials before a (yet to be scheduled) end of term party in week 12. See the yellow row above, which shows the teo-week period in which tutorials should be scheduled.

We are asking you to discuss #CreativeCoding activity with the students during these sessions across term. Get to know them a little by discussing and engaging with the concepts that they learn about, data they collect and the portfolios that they make. Prompt them, ask them, see what they are up to - what they can and can't do, what they find challenging.

There is a "getting to know you" angle in the first term - getting to know yourself and each other through data and its creative representation - inspired by Giorgia Lupi & Stef Posavec's wonderful Dear Data project.
The students' will produce Dear Data 100 demos across term, and learn the Processing code to do so in simple 'sketches'. They will need to collect 100 data items about something that is meaningful to them and visualize these.
This will tell us something about their activity and interests as they collect data about themselves through term. Keen tutors may be so overwhelmingly excited by all of this that they decide to participate in the exercise themselves.

At the end of term you will be asked to produce a short summary of your tutees' efforts: a Creative Coding Commentary that involves a few sentences describing their initiative, participation, creativity, & group-work capability in the #CreativeCoding activity and first term of tutorials.

This is intended to motivate students and provide a few useful reminders, that have a personal angle, for future reference writing. It is generously workloaded in the WAM.

This document explains what is required of you as a personal tutor, outlining the minimum that is required and and offers some ideas that may inspire you to take things a little further. It makes no mention at all of medals: either real or imaginary.

The Minimum

Meet the students as requested above, comment on their code and sketches, get to know them a little and write a short report for each at the end of term. Come to the end of term event.

As you can see, we have FOUR Personal Tutor check-ins at the start, mid and end of term. These are our pastoral sessions with a "Show Me Your SketchBook" and "Me in Data" angle.

1week 02 or 03
50 mins		Group session to make sure that all students understand the Dear Data 100 task and the technology and have started to experiment with data collection on an appropriate theme.
2week 04 or 05
15 mins each		Individual session to discuss sketchbooks, show an interactive method and evidence of some data (and a settled data theme).
3week 08 or 09
15 mins each		Individual session to discuss progress, explain a sketch that involves an object (and a class), that data collection is progressing and to discuss the sketchbook and ideas for the planned data visualization.
4week 10 or 11
50 mins		Group session to share, compare and discuss sketches, data sets, progress and ambition.

The minimum we ask is that you connect with your tutees and book meetings as scheduled.
They should all be in the same practical class group and you should be able to see this scheduled on MyTimetable and so work around it.

The group tutorials will be organised by Edward as the induction week ones were and timetabled.
You will need to arrange the individual tutorials yourself.
We recommend using the Bookings feature in Office, which works extremely well. It's also a great idea to set up a group containing all of your tutees, so that you can communicate with them reliably and easily.

We suggest that you book the individual tutorials for this term right away and get them in your diary and the students' diaries.

Tutorial 1 - Group (50 mins - ish)

All you need to do in weeks 02 or 03 is say Hello, do some introductions and ask the students what they need to do in #CreativeCoding. Let them explain their learning.

They should be trying to come up with a Dear Data 100 focus, collecting data about their lives that is based around some topic:

  • the topic should be original and unique - they have been given some creativity techniques, get them to explain and use these
  • the topic must be something that interests and / or is meaningful to them - ask them why it matters! Get them to discuss this and what they are collecting about the topic - and how it is going! What are they trying to find out?
  • the data must not reveal identifying personal information - be anonymous you can advise on this and discuss potential for identity leak

Please help them make a judgment here - pretty much anything goes! Just be encouraging.
Ask them to explain why this is meaningful to them, why it is likely to be original and to reassure you about steps taken to ensure anonymity.

If you are unable to answer anything, or receive questions about #CreativeCoding, then remind students about the Creative Coding Discussion Forum on Moodle or get them to contact me in an email with CC25 in the subject field please.

Please send questions about this activity and / or feedback once it has happened to me with CC25 in the subject field. Constructive feedback would be very much appreciated.

Tutorial 2 - Individual (15 mins)

This is an individual session - spend 15 minutes with each tutee discussing their progress.

It would be good to see a sketch or too (laptops out, pre-warn them about this) and ask about their progress.
We would like them to show an interactive method that they have produced and demonstrate evidence of some data collection for their developing Dear Data 100 sketch - encourage them to settle on a theme.

Please remind them that they will receive a personal paragraph that describes their approach to and engagement with the #CreativeCoding activity. You are looking to see initiative, participation, creativity, and group-work capability in the tutorials and would like to write something positive that captures their abilities, approach and engagement, and that is personal - relying upon their Dear Data 100 ideas.

Encourage them to use Git and ask them about their Git repository (and maybe give them a nudge). There is a Git Guide with videos and the City Computer Science Society also provides Git Session tutorials.

Now, of course this may lead to, or be overtaken by, more pressing personal tutorial issues ... and in a way, this is the point. #CreativeCoding is a bit of an icebreaker, a way to connect with the students and for students to get to know tutors. Use your judgment to decide where to focus the tutorial, you can always ask them back during office hours or send them to the TA practical labs, but do try to encourage the students to participate in #CreativeCoding if you can - and remind them of the next tutorials in weeks 08 or 09 when they will need to show their data depicted in objects. We introduce some exciting capabilities for using sound and video in their sketches in week 07, so they will need to be up to date with the learning material by then - and have some data! Encourage them to prepare for this - the materials are already online on Moodle.

Incidentally, if students are not engaging, and not all of them will, then chat about other tings, including motivation. This page on What's The Point?! might provide some prompts

Tips for Engagement

How to get your students to come to the 1:1 tutorials!

  1. Check MyTimetable to find gaps in their timetable when they (and you) are available.
  2. Then use the Outlook Bookings feature to allow them to book a 15 minute session with you during this time - you can set this up to send reminders half an hour beforehand.
    I find 'use customised availability hours' particularly useful as you can be very specific about when meetings can occur
  3. In Outlook you can create a group for your tutees and send a message just to this group - Outlook Groups. Outlook automatically filters it into a folder with the group name.
    My tutees saw the message because it appeared in the group, not in their overfull inbox (which contains all the discussion messages sent in every module and so is largely unusable!).
Tutorial 3 - Individual (15 mins)

This is a second individual session - spend 15 minutes with each tutee, using some prompting around their progress to get to know them and enable them to raise any issues that they would like to discuss.

Ask them to show you their sketchbook and in particular discuss a sketch that contains an object (and a class) that they have written.

Hopefully this will be a class that enables them to show some of the data that they have been collecting. Some discussion around the concepts of classes and instances, and how their class models and represents their data, which models and represents some aspect of The World might ensue. Or you could talk about something completely different - just be your usual friendly, open, accessible self and reinforce your position as their academic point of contact.

If they have not made much progress, remind them that it is very straightforward to create a simple class and that the Robots Example Page explains it on a step by step basis.

Check that data collection is progressing and discuss their developing sketchbook and ultimate ideas for data visualization - which they may sketch for you in their notebooks, or on your whiteboard.

Outcomes & Aims

One thing that will probably kill your interest is a set of learning objectives, but don't scarper just yet, we need to know collectively what the point is, and we have tried to keep it simple. So have a look!

  • #CreativeCoding Objectives

There is a role here for Personal Tutors - to do some of the encouraging and provide feedback on progress.

And what is the point of the #CreativeCoding / Personal Tutorial crossover? Broadly ...

  • to build relationships between students and tutors
    letting us get to know them, letting them get to know us - around some self-identified theme(s)
  • to generate evidence of their capabilities for when we need to write references for employers or further study
    this is intended to be an efficient way of establishing and remembering who the students are and giving us something to go on at reference time
  • to reframe concepts of data a little and have some fun
    discussing portfolios of sketches with creative approaches to data collection and representation might actually be interesting, thought provoking and enjoyable.

Dear Data & Dear Data 100

You may also want to know a little about the #CreativeCoding activity, the Dear Data Project and the Dear Data 100" demos that students will be developing, showing and sharing.
A quick way in is to invest a little time in these three short resources that collectively involve about 15 minutes of reading & watching.

  • The Dear Data Introduction - the start of the book, explains the project succinctly and we've digitised it so it's accessible online to all;
  • The to DRAW is to REMEMBER annotated guidance from the book that persuades us that "IT'S not THAT HARD" to participate in a Dear Data 100 project and may be persuasive and motivating for students;
  • The short (3 min) Dear Data (Big Bang Data) video outlines the original project and provides some valuable context;

Incidentally, the TALIS Reading Lists that we have associated with each module work superbly and are an excellent and efficient resource. Selecting, annotating and prioritising resources seems to me to be absolutely core teaching activity, that is extremely useful for students. Letting them know what they should read, and why, and giving them access to it is a fantastic way of getting our knowledge to them. If you don't produce annotated, ordered reading lists, then I strongly encourage you to do so - it works extremely well and the library provide great support.

Dear Data 100

You might decide to collect some personal data yourself and make a visualization in your own Dear Data 100 project.
Discuss it with your students - what does it reveal. Be creative. Be inspiring! Reveal yourself in data with your students and compare your experiences and discoveries.

So, make yourself a Dear Data 100 sketch, programme or artefact and engage with the students by going through their process.
You don't need to use Processing - you could use another language or package, or even an analogue approach: postcards, whiteboards, PostIts, lego ... be creative!
This might be rewarding and should be fun.

Each pair of postcards produced over the Dear Data year is available on the the By Week ‚Äî Dear Data page - be inspired by these examples, experiences, reflections.
