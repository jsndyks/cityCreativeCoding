Year 1 Personal Tutorials & #CreativeCoding Commentary Task

Short Story

Context

You'll remember that students have been asked to produce ...

A Processing sketch
that depicts a data set that they have collected over time
that explores some aspect of the World
that is interesting to them

Hopefully ...

in creative & expressive ways
that they are pleased with and
that helps them learn (a little) about themselves & The World around them

... and that if they do this and a couple of other things, they have been promised get a Digital Badge for #CreativeCoding that they can display and share on social media along with a short summary of their efforts: a Creative Coding Commentary?

Action

Well, 100 students completed the voluntary task, about 1/4 of them.

Tutors now need write a few sentences describing each of the submitting students' initiative, participation, creativity, & group-work capability in the #CreativeCoding activity and tutorials. This is the Creative Coding Commentary that we promised that Stage 1 Personal Tutors would produce.

We need a few short sentences that are intended to provide them with some encouragement, and you with some colour and individual 'data' to help you (or a colleague) with convincing reference writing down the line.

Full Story

Here's How To Do It ...

The Workload Model gives us 20 mins per tutee for this task, so we hope that you can use this time to ...

  1. Check the List of Submitting Students to see who has submitted from your Stage 1 Tutor Group
  2. Have a look at the Dear Data Sketch Submissions for each of these students;
  3. Draft a brief commentary, based upon the submission and your engagement with them through the process;
  4. Log the commentary on e:Vision;
  5. Send a brief e-mail to the students to let them know that you have completed the commentary, with a copy of the text, any feedback on the submission and some congratulations on having submitted. If you are going to the Pizza & Data Party then encourage them to come along.

Details follow ...

Submitted Files

You'll find two folders for each students who has submitted here:

  • Dear Data Sketch Submissions

These are ...

  • StudentName_ ... _onlinetext,
    A web page that offers a short commentary on the effort.
    This should be in a file called onlinetext.html - click and see!
  • StudentName_ ... _file, which contains:
    • a screen dump - so you can see something of what was produced.
      This may be all you need;
    • an archive - containing code and data, that you can unpack (double click?), run and play with.
      Do this if you like, no need.
      It might run in Processing if you are lucky.
      If it doesn't - then make sure that filename.pde is in a folder called filename.
      So ccProj.pde must be in a ccProj folder (./ccProj/ccProj.pde)
      If that doesn't work ... give up.
Drafting the Text

This is just a chance to say something that is positive, personal and compact about the student and their efforts that you (or somebody else) might draw upon when writing a reference.

Focus on their initiative, participation, creativity, & group-work capability in the #CreativeCoding activity and the tutorials that you ran.
Try to be specific about their choice of topic and data collection, and any reflection that occurred.

The four examples that I have written are copied below (anonymised) in an Appendix.
Feel free to use words, structure or nothing at all from these to inspire your efforts.

Give as much or as little detail as you like in your commentaries and emails.

Student 1 : Phone Shapes
Creative Coding Commentary

S1 participated well in our first year Creative Coding activity, a voluntary course that required (and encouraged) creative thinking and relied upon some personal data collection and autonomous study.

She/he attended and contributed well to tutorials and showed some capability as a creative coder - we used Processing to learn to code and produce output quickly that helped stimulate discussion about data and self.

She/he produced a Dear Data Diary showing his/her phone use over a week and used classes to capture, display and analyze this information effectively in an original interactive sketch. There were some nice ideas in his/her visualization, which was elegant, expressive and revealing - Friday seemed to be particularly busy time!

S1 was a committed, capable and motivated creative coder and his/her efforts helped him/her to reflect on the notion of data as "material" that is a starting point for play, expression and learning in ways that the Dear Data book encourages.

E-Mail
Hi S1, nice work!

I like the combination of shapes and colors … and wonder whether we can see anything in here?
Lots of blue? Not much red? Not many circles?

I like the way shape complexity is ordered to show day sequence, but is this easy to detect and interpret in the graphic? I’m not sure. 

The code looks pretty good - nice use of a class and drawPolygon() is an elegant solution.

Ways to improve things :

 - get the Shape functionality into the Shape class - draw() etc.
   Objects can contain data and methods
 - separate data from code - see the notes and use loadStrings();
 - try to remove numbers from your code - use variables or parameters (e.g. clickCount < 7 could be clickCount < 7 ArrayList.size())
 - make sure you  understand ArrayLists - I think you only need one of these here
 - the randomness makes it interesting, but could you check on shape positions to avoid overlap?
 - a legend and explanation would be handy!

You can probably think of other things too.

Well done on submitting such a nice piece of work.
I hope you got the CC Badge and will come and show your work at the Pizza & Data Party.

Jason.

Hi S4,

Great idea, and a compelling visualization despite your data set being pretty small. Nevertheless you showed some excellent creative coding - highlights including:

    - the use of classes;
    - the separation of data and code;
    - interaction and animation;
    - it looks great!;

Your flowers capture lots of data and you use ArrayLists and loops and conditions very effectively.
Employing animation (rotation) as a means of conveying information was very creative and worked nicely.

Ways to improve things :

    - you could use methods in your class to do some of the drawing and scaling, objects contain functionality as well as data - drawFlower() for example might have been a method in Flower called with flower.drawFlower();
    - there is quite a lot of action in keyPressed() and we would normally try to just change state variables in there and act on these in the main draw() loop;
    - constraining the flowers to the screen might have been a good idea (maybe)
    - some more data might have made a more compelling visualization

I’m sure you can think of other things as well!

This really was an impressive piece of work.
