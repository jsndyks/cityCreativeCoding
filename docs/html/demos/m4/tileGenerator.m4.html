This project involves using Processing to generate tiled patterns, or tessellations. Tiles can be easily generated with Processing since it involves using loops to repeatedly draw simple shapes. By varying the shape drawn and the colours used to fill the shapes, attractive patterns can be produced.

Tessellations are space-filling shapes that fit together to form sometimes intricate patterns. There is a huge variety of possible tessellations and this project involves you getting creative with Processing to produce some visually pleasing tiling patterns. For inspiration you might like to try doing a Google image search for tessellated tiling

Square Grids

A simple way of starting would be to create a regular grid of square tiles. You might chose to colour these in interesting ways. To create a regular grid in Processing, you can use nested for loops - one counting through the rows in your grid, the other inside it counting through the columns.

You can add further interest by adding a small random variation to each corner or vertex of the shape you are drawing. While strictly not a tessellation (because it can lead to gaps and overlaps), it can produce a visually pleasing effect.


Interesting patterns can be generated even from the most basic of shapes. For example, by randomly choosing between four orientations of square tiles made up of two triangles each, a so-called Truchet tiling can be generated.

Think about what other shapes you might choose to draw. For example, by replacing the four triangular tiles with two tiles composed of simple arcs of circles (generated in Processing with the arc() method) a curved maze effect can be created.




Using fill colours for the quarter circles gives rise to four combinations from which solid maze-like paths can be created.




Hexagonal Grids

You can consider further possibilities by arranging shapes on a hexagonal rather than square grid. The principle is much the same in that you will also need to use nested loops to iterate through the rows and columns of the grid. The only difference is that every other row is slightly offset from the row above and below it.

It is often useful, before you start coding, to sketch out your design on paper first. So for example, to create a cube-like tessellation based on a hexagonal grid, you might end up sketching something like the following.


This cube shape is built up by drawing a diamond shape (using Processing's quad() method.) and then rotating the diamond clockwise by 120 degrees to give the yellow right face, then another 120 degrees to give the red left face. You will probably find it easiest to put the code to draw a single tile (like this cube) in its own method so you can call it multiple times from inside your nested for loops. This also makes it easier to modify your tile to produce different visual effects.

For this project, you can design your own choice of tiles and colouring. Some possible variations include those below, but you are encouraged also to come up with your own ideas.

  • Introduce some random colouring in your tiles. Think about your colour choice to enhance the appearance of the tessellation.
  • Introduce a random choice of different tiles and see the effect of combining them.
  • How might you use interaction (from the mouse or keyboard) to influence the sketch?
  • Consider animating your tessellation, for example by gradually changing the size or orientation of the tiles.
