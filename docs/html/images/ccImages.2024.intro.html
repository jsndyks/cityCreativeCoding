2. What is an image?

For our purposes, an image is a two-dimensional array, or regular grid, of colours. One can think of these as grid of miniature coloured rectangles positioned next to one another on the screen. They are much like a mosaic of tiles, each tile being of a single colour.

Mosaics are great - they allow us to create physical images and decorate our floors, bathrooms, window ledges ...

If you look carefully when you visit Paris, you'll see occasional tile mosaics made by Invader and various others who have followed this idea.

Jim Bachor's amazing pothole mosaics are gaining some traction too, though, with some exceptions, these tend not to use regular grids.

To store regular grid mosaics digitally, we record information about the colour in each tile, and where each tile is positioned in the grid. The more tiles, the more detail.

We don't refer to these individually coloured elements of the image as tiles though. We refer to them as picture elements - or pixels for short.

And you will already have some ideas about how we specify colours digitally.

2.1 - Image resolution

We refer to the number of pixels contained in an image as its resolution.
The more pixels, the more detail, the higher resolution.

The numbers of pixels in these example images increases from the top left (3x3) to bottom right (300x300).

You should see that there is more detail in the image as we increase the number of pixels used, thereby increasing the resolution of the image.

The white gaps between pixels in these examples are just there to help show the grid structure - we would remove them when displaying or printing an image.

2.3 - 256 Shades of grey

Extending the range of shades that we use, from a binary light | dark distinction to a sequence of greys, also allows us to increase the amount of information in the image.

Here we have the same numbers of pixels as in the two-shade images above, but use 256 different shades of grey, rather than two.

These images again use 9x9, 30x30, 100x100 and 300x300 pixels from left to right.

But this time each pixel contains one of 255 shades of grey, from light color(255) through to dark color(0).

In this example our colour resolution has increased from 2 to 256 shades.
Once again our image resolution increases from left to right.

2.4 - 16 Million Colours

We add colour to our images by recording three numbers, rather than just one, for each pixel. These are the amounts of red, green, and blue present at each pixel. Combining these three colour components, or channels as they are sometimes called, allows us to describe colours very precisely.
Specifying the red, green and blue components of any colour on a scale of 0-255 allows us to differentiate between 256ãŽ¥ colours. That's more than 16 million different colour combinations. This is plenty in most circumstances.

These images all use a 30x30 grid, so they have a resolution of 900 pixels.

The other three images show the amounts of red, green and blue stored in each pixel of the image to the left.
Each colour component ranges from 0 to 255.
